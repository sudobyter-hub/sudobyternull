$ Active Directory, in plain words

An apartment building has a doorman with a tenant ledger. Active Directory is that doorman — for computers. By the end of this page you'll understand why it exists, how it actually works, and the five attacks every red-teamer reaches for first.

why AD exists at all#

Imagine you own a tiny company with three employees and three laptops. Setting up usernames is easy: you make a user account on each laptop. Three people, three machines, nine accounts. Whatever.

Now imagine the company grows to three thousand employees. Five offices. Two thousand laptops. Forty servers. A few hundred printers. Some VPN gateways. A wiki. A file share. A code repo.

Without a central directory, every single one of those machines has its own list of who can log in. A new hire on Monday means making accounts on… everything. An employee leaving on Friday means hunting them down on every machine they ever touched. This is unmanageable inside a week.

analogy

Picture a 50-story apartment building. Without a front-desk ledger, every shop, gym, and laundry room in the building has to keep its own list of "who's allowed in." Move in, move out, change your name — you'd have to walk to every shop and update them yourself.

AD is the building's doorman. There's one ledger. Every business inside the building trusts the doorman. You show ID to the doorman once, and from then on the doorman vouches for you.

That's it. That's the whole reason AD exists. One identity, trusted everywhere on the network. Everything else — the protocols, the database, the attacks — is just the machinery that makes that promise real.

the cast of characters#

AD has its own vocabulary. Most of it is intimidating until you map it to the apartment building.

  • Domain — one building. Has a name like corp.local. Everyone inside trusts the same doorman.
  • Forest — a city block of buildings that all know each other. A trust relationship lets a tenant in one building enter another.
  • Organizational Unit (OU) — a floor inside a building. Lets the doorman organize tenants by department.
  • User — an actual person who can log in.
  • Group — a label. "Sales", "Domain Admins", "VPN Users". You give permissions to a group, then drop users into it.
  • Computer — yes, computers have accounts too. They authenticate to the domain just like users do.
  • Domain Controller (DC) — the doorman himself. The actual server running AD. There's usually more than one for redundancy.

When red-teamers say "I got Domain Admin", they mean: they hold credentials for a user whose group membership lets them do anything inside a domain. We'll come back to why that's the magic phrase in §6.

the phonebook — LDAP#

AD stores all that structure — domains, OUs, users, groups, computers, attributes, permissions — in one giant searchable database. The way you query that database is called LDAP (Lightweight Directory Access Protocol).

LDAP is just a phonebook with a query language. Every "find me…" question you can ask of AD turns into an LDAP query. For example, "find me every account whose name ends in -admin":

# filter on the sAMAccountName attribute ldapsearch -H ldap://dc01.corp.local -D "alice@corp.local" -W \ -b "DC=corp,DC=local" \ "(sAMAccountName=*-admin)" sAMAccountName memberOf

The very first thing a red-teamer with a foothold does is query LDAP. Who are the Domain Admins? What computers exist? What service accounts have weird permissions? Which users haven't logged in for two years? All of it is one phonebook lookup. None of it is "hacking" — it's just reading. And by default, any authenticated user can read most of the directory.

Tools like BloodHound automate this: they fire thousands of LDAP queries, build a graph of "who can reach what," and hand you the shortest path to Domain Admin.

the ticket counter — Kerberos#

OK so AD knows who everyone is. How do you actually prove you're a user when you sit down at a laptop and try to open a file share? Sending your password to every server in the building is a terrible idea. Instead, AD uses Kerberos.

analogy

Picture a museum with a front desk and lots of separate exhibition halls. You show your ID at the front desk once in the morning. The front desk hands you a master wristband. Every time you want to enter an exhibition, you trade your wristband for an exhibition-specific ticket. The exhibition checks the ticket and lets you in. They never see your ID.

That's it. That's Kerberos. Names matter, so:

  • The front desk is the KDC (Key Distribution Center). It lives on the Domain Controller. It has two sub-windows: the AS (Authentication Service) and the TGS (Ticket-Granting Service).
  • The master wristband is the TGT (Ticket-Granting Ticket). You get it once at login.
  • The exhibition-specific ticket is the service ticket (or TGS ticket). One per service you want to talk to.

Here's the full handshake:

Three things to remember about this:

  1. The TGT is encrypted with the KDC's secret key — a key that, on the DC, is derived from the password of a built-in account called krbtgt. Remember krbtgt. It'll come back.
  2. Each service ticket is encrypted with that service's password hash. If you steal a service ticket and the service's password is weak, you can crack the ticket offline and recover the password.
  3. The system trusts encryption, not the network. Anyone who steals or forges the right encrypted blob is that user, as far as the service knows.

Those three facts are where every Kerberos attack comes from.

where it breaks#

Now you know enough to actually understand the five attacks. Each one bends a property we just covered. I'll give you the plain-language version first, then the technical name.

"Ask the front desk for a service ticket, then go home and try to crack the ticket's encryption to recover the service's password." You don't need to use the ticket — you just need to be holding one. The KDC will hand a TGS ticket to anyone who's already authenticated and asks for one. If the service has a weak password, the encryption is weak.

→ Kerberoasting. Targets service accounts (any user with a Service Principal Name).

"Ask the front desk for someone else's master wristband, but for an account whose pre-auth check is turned off." Normally the AS won't issue a TGT unless you can prove you know the user's password (the pre-auth step). Some legacy accounts have pre-auth disabled. For those, you get the AS to hand you a TGT blob — and that blob is encrypted with their password hash, so you can crack it offline.

→ AS-REP Roasting. Targets accounts with DONT_REQ_PREAUTH set.

"Stand in the hallway between a user's laptop and a server. When the user goes to authenticate, secretly forward their authentication to a different server you actually want to log into." The user's machine politely answers an NTLM challenge for you. You replay it elsewhere.

→ NTLM Relay. Works against the older NTLM protocol that AD still supports for compatibility.

"Politely ask the doorman for a copy of everyone's passwords, by pretending you're another doorman doing routine replication." DCs replicate the directory between themselves. If you have a specific replication right — a permission a couple of accounts have by default and others get by accident — you can ask one DC to send you the password hashes of every user in the domain.

→ DCSync. Requires the Replicating Directory Changes right. Game over if you have it.

"Forge your own master wristband, because you stole the rubber stamp the front desk uses to make them." Remember krbtgt's key — the one used to encrypt every TGT? If you ever steal it (usually via DCSync), you can generate TGTs for any user, with any group membership, valid for 10 years by default. The KDC will accept them.

→ Golden Ticket. Persistence on hard mode. The fix is rotating krbtgt's password — twice.

Every one of those is the same shape: identity is encrypted blobs, and we found a way to grab or forge one. Once you see the pattern, every new "AD attack" is a variation.

what "Domain Admin" really means#

Domain Admin is a group. Members of it are allowed to do anything inside the domain: read every password hash, run code on every machine, modify the directory, create new accounts, change group memberships. There are a few other groups in the neighborhood — Enterprise Admins (the whole forest), Schema Admins (the database structure itself) — but DA is the one that matters in practice.

Why is it the holy grail? Because once you have it:

  • You can read every user's password hash → log in as anyone.
  • You can DCSync → cement persistence with a Golden Ticket.
  • You can run code on any machine joined to the domain → ransomware, data exfil, whatever the engagement scope allows.
  • You can quietly add yourself to the group on a different account → backdoor for later.

That's why the very first thing BloodHound shows you, when you point it at a network, is a node colored bright red labeled DOMAIN ADMINS and the shortest path from your foothold to it.

what attackers actually do#

Real engagements rarely look like a single exploit. They look like a chain. The same chain shows up in real breaches, in CTFs, in pentest reports — once you've seen it twice, you'll see it everywhere.

Plain version:

  1. Get in. Phishing email, exposed VPN, a CVE on the Citrix box facing the internet. Almost never AD's fault.
  2. Look around. Run BloodHound or roll your own LDAP queries. Build a map of users, groups, computers, ACLs.
  3. Get credentials. Kerberoast every service account, AS-REP-roast every legacy account, crack the hashes offline. Maybe dump lsass.exe on a workstation for cached creds. Maybe NTLM-relay your way to a server's machine account.
  4. Move sideways. Use the new creds to log into other machines. Repeat steps 2-3 from each new vantage point.
  5. Get to a Domain Admin. Sometimes you find one's cached creds. Sometimes BloodHound shows you a chain of "user → owns → group → owns → DA" that lets you walk there through misconfigured permissions.
  6. DCSync. Pull all the password hashes. Forge a Golden Ticket. You now have undetected, decade-long persistence in the environment.

going deeper#

If this clicked and you want more, here's where to keep going. None of these need money — but they each take time.

read

  • The "Active Directory Hardening" series by SpecterOps — the people behind BloodHound. Single best technical writing on AD security.
  • Will Schroeder's blog (harmj0y.net) — the foundational papers on Kerberoasting, AS-REP, ACL abuse. Dense, worth it.
  • "AD Attack Defense" by Sean Metcalf (adsecurity.org) — from a defender's angle, which is the same angle just facing the other way.

practice

  • HackTheBox Pro Labs — Dante, Offshore, Cybernetics. Multi-machine AD networks. The closest thing to a real engagement you can do in a browser.
  • TryHackMe — search "Active Directory" — there's a whole curated path.
  • GOAD (Game of Active Directory) — a free, automated lab that spins up a vulnerable forest on your laptop. Set it up once and break it for weeks.

tools to actually know

  • BloodHound + SharpHound — the map.
  • impacket suite (secretsdump, GetUserSPNs, GetNPUsers, psexec.py) — the swiss army.
  • Rubeus — Kerberos on Windows hosts.
  • mimikatz — credential theft. Legendary, still works.
  • CrackMapExec / NetExec — the sledgehammer for spraying creds across hosts.

AD is huge — there's a lifetime of detail past this page. But everything you'll read from here on out fits onto the scaffold above: identity → directory → tickets → encrypted blobs → blobs that can be stolen or forged. If you ever feel lost in a paper, anchor back to one of those.
